Dans Rajas of the Ganges, on se retrouve dans l’Inde à l’époque de l’empire moghol. Cette puissance en plein essor étend son territoire et gagne en prospérité grâce au commerce de la soie, du thé et des épices.

Des monuments imposants tels que le Taj Mahal ou le Fort Rouge sont érigés. De somptueux jardins sont créés, ainsi que de nouveaux édifices et parcs, qui émerveilleront les générations futures au cours des siècles à venir.

Alors que l’empire a pratiquement atteint son étendue maximale et se trouve dans une période de relative stabilité, les joueurs vont incarner des rajas ou des ranis et s’efforcer de se montrer à la hauteur de leur rang de vénérables souverains.

Pour cela, ils devront transformer leurs terres en provinces opulentes et prestigieuses. Des dés, mais peu de hasard !

Un 1 n’est pas toujours une mauvaise chose vue que chaque chiffre apporte son pouvoir spécial et que l’on peut utiliser son karma pour prendre la valeur du dé de l’autre face. Le 1 passant ainsi à 6 peut faire bien du changement dans votre stratégie !

N’oublions pas la joie d’échanger un jet de dé qui nous sert à rien contre 2 autres dés de couleurs différentes ce qui peut nous ouvrir une myriades de possibilités…

L’importance de bien choisir sa voie
Rapidement on se rend compte qu’avec toutes les options possibles, on ne peut pas être partout et trop se diviser c’est s’assurer de perdre car on sera faible dans toutes les sphères.

Les voies possibles
Commençons par le cœur du royaume, les merveilles du monde, on en a 4, chacune à son importance quand on décide de bâtir son royaume en achetant des tuiles. Il faudra donc prévoir d’avance ce que l’on veut acheter comme tuile pour qu’elle nous fasse gagner le plus de points possible.

Ensuite il y a les 3 précieuses ressources, il faudra ici encore décider le plus rapidement possible sur quoi l’on veut baser sa stratégie: la diversité ou la spécialisation dans une ressource qui pourra nous rapporter gros ?

Le long fleuve n’est pas dénué d’intérêt non plus, il nous apportera tout au long de la partie des bonus non négligeables, il faut juste faire attention de bien compter ses déplacements qui nous font avance de la valeur du dés sur les espaces VIDES, détail qui a toute son importance !

En dernier recours il y aura toujours la possibilité d’utiliser un ouvrier pour récupérer un dé d’une couleur. Certes c’est très peu, mais parfois on aura besoin de tout utiliser pour faire de grandes choses; il faut juste savoir comment rebondir rapidement à défaut de perdre un tour entier pour se refaire…

Intérêt :
C’est un bon placement d’ouvrier avec une belle course au point qui va dans les deux sens. Le premier joueur qui réussit à faire croiser son niveau de prestige avec sa richesse met fin à la partie et bien souvent il risque de la remporter sauf si la course est bien serré.

On a bien aimé Rajas of the Ganges pour cet aspect, c’est bien fait et cela fait changement des autres placements d’ouvriers qui ont souvent tendances à nous faire jouer un nombre x de tours avant la fin de la partie.

Ici on ne sait pas quand la partie va réellement terminer et cela ajoute donc un certain stress et importance de faire ses actions au tour présent pour ne pas se faire avoir en prévoyant trop d’avance.

Le niveau de difficulté n’est pas très élevé et on peu facilement croire qu’après une partie on saura comment mieux s’y prendre et donc rejouer pour s’améliorer sera un plaisir.

La belle variété des actions font de Rajas of the Ganges un choix que je conseil plus qu’un jeu comme Little Town et Stone Age qui sont tout les deux de bons jeux, mais avec un niveau de redondance beaucoup plus grand.

You can't have it all I thought when I placed the pieces of Rajas of the Ganges back in the box at the end of a board game night last night. It was one of my favorite games until an hour before.

When I explain the game to others I always tell them that Rajas of The Ganges is set in 16th century India during the period when the mighty empire of the Great Moguls was developing and more specifically in the area between the rivers Indus and Ganges. The players in this game take on the role of Rajas or Ranis - influential nobles of the country - and compete with each other in amassing both spiritual and tangible wealth while simultaneously trying to develop their estates into wealthy and magnificent provinces.
During the game, players use both dice and money to perform their actions. Sometimes one, sometimes the other, sometimes both and always supported by the placement of one of their workers. They will have to plan these actions carefully because they cannot always place their workers in the places they want. Also interesting is the use of karma in this game, which makes impossible actions suddenly possible again.
I fell in love with this game because of the artwork. When I saw that beautiful box, with all those cheerful colors, I wanted to have the game. I hadn't touched anything yet, hadn't made a move yet. I just had to own this.

And when I'd bought it and having it in front of me, at home, drooling over the cardboard, my gut feeling immediately clicked.

As it turned out, the game was designed by Markus and Inka Brandt (who are also responsible for many escape games plus the world hit Village). That gave confidence.

Even more important was my click with the artwork. That turned out to be from Dennis Lohausen. We know them from the big games by Uwe Rosenberg, but also from First Rat. Big favorites in the Mrs. Verschuur Arena. Lohausen is a man who has mastered several illustration styles. Completely blinded by the overwhelming sight, I hadn't even noticed.
But... it was completely understandable that I fell for it and so I had nothing to be ashamed of.

Although beautiful to see when it is all laid out in front of you, I will no longer claim that it is always a joy to play Rajas of the Ganges.
First of all, the dice provide a luck factor in the game that should not be underestimated. I initially persistently downplayed this until I recently played a game in which I threw ones and twos almost constantly. Then I had a different story. In addition, the available or unavailable landscape tiles are also a thing. If there isn't anything that suits you, then you're out of luck.

However, all this pales in comparison to the "tactics" that seasoned heavy players use when the game is about halfway through. This tactic means that you have three markets (tea, spices, silk) on your estate, each with a value of 3. This allows you to send a worker to the market on each turn, scoring 9 free points each time.

This method takes all the joy out of the game. Especially when there are more players who use this, the game degenerates into a rather vulgar and joyless point-buying race.

Why am I telling this?
Well, dear board game enthusiasts, it just so happens that I took Rajas to a Games Club yesterday (in preparation for an appointment I have later this week). It was even more coincidental that one of my fellow players warned us three times before the start of the game about this extremely annoying and base way of playing.

I thought that was very sympathetic of him and immediately thought: "Oh, this gentleman is aware of that annoying method and is just as disgusted by it as I am."
This made me feel safe, made the gentleman in question even more sympathetic and made me want to play even more than I initially had.
However, my deep disappointment was when this figure (sorry for the expression, but I can't make anything else of it) not only applied this tactic himself, but also encouraged his girlfriend to do the same.
The game therefore ended in a boring and joyless run for the points by two players, which reminded me of the traumas of my darkest Monopoly days.
For the game and the artwork I give Rajas 10 big stars. I'll deduct a star for the luck factor and the points race.

Título: Ganges - Are the Goodie boxes worth it?
Contenido:
Rajas is a dice rolling and worker placement game, where you pay with dice and sometimes money for an action. It looks like a lot because the board is super busy, but it really isn't so bad

The base game has already been reviewed by many, so I will quickly tell you about the expansions:

Tiger, cow and snake tiles: each of them add a new tile to the respective stacks with an immediate bonus and a continuous bonus. They cost 2 of the same colour dice or one specific colour and one extra one.
-The cow tiles give you extra money for the market actions (and money when you place them)
-With the snake tiles you can take a worker back from the board and replace it somewhere else (so another action), nothing else happens
-The tiger tiles are all different and grant you for example a bonus where you can trade one die for two other dice if you go to the Palace. Or one extra step when sailing with the boat

Mango Village are also new tiles that give you bonuses. They cost two of the same valued dice (can be different colours). When placed they give you a bonus, but when activated they give you the bonus again. You can activate up to two of them by going to the specific mango Market, which is a new location on the board

The karma track now has a new board as well, where you can place your worker and take a bonus of an unoccupied karma space. Front side: 1 fame, 3 coins, 1 upgrade or 1 die (back side: 1 fame per current karma level, 1 boat step, sell 2 goods or a white bonus token)

Next to the karma track is a new track, where you can place a worker to gain coins for the 3 tiles next to your Palace. The backside does the same, but now you also have to roll a die and depending on the number you rolled and the tiles on your player board, you get coins.

There are new river tiles and two ways to play them (see the rulebook for this). We played with them face down and however arrives first gets a coin for exploring

And the pink elephant, but unfortunately we didn't use it. Whoever is on the reroll spot can take the pink elephant and reuse a spot that the starting player has occupied. It can also be stolen, but we wouldn't know..

Then there are the Tokens from the Holi festival, which are placed on the score tracks. However reaches these tiles first takes one die of each colour and rolls them. The last player gets to pick first, followed by the second to last etc. The last player also gets to use this tile as a one-time joker tile, where they can pretend a die is of a different colour

The trader is a new worker that replaces another worker. This worker is placed at fame 10. If you get the worker, you can make the die you use for that action, into any pip value you want! This one is super strong!

And the last one (I think), is the one with the starting tiles. You have forest, water, desert or mountain, each with their own bonus. You get one random one and have to place it in one of four specific locations on your board. It just makes you reach the sides earlier, but as you place it you also receive a bonus.

All in all a fun game and (most of) the expansions make it more fun! So yes, to me they are worth it, although the base game already offers enough for me as it is. I will not add them to every play, but it is a good way to keep the game interesting.

: HUCH!
: Inka & Markus Brand
: Dennis Lohausen

: Dice Rolling, Network and Route Building, Race, Tile Placement & Worker Placement

You’re a raja in 16th century India who wants to develop his estate into a wealthy province. You’ll try to achieve this by sending out workers and accumulate dice to gather fame and wealth. During his turn a player sends out a worker to one of the locations on the board and, after paying the required money or dice, carries out the accompanying action. The game ends when a player’s markers on the fame and money tracks, which run in opposite directions, pass each other. The player with the biggest difference between both markers wins.

Compliments to the designers for the iconography used in Rajas of the Ganges. Though the game board is beautifully illustrated, it looks very crowded and overwhelming at first. After a few turns the iconography really comes true and makes everything seem logical. Besides worker and tile placement, which we love, Rajas of the Ganges uses dice as a resource to pay for certain actions. The value of your dice is determined by rolling said dice and can cause some real joy or frustration.

We like that everything in Rajas of the Ganges is connected. Sending your worker to a location causes additional actions, bonuses and opens up other possibilities. We also like that you earn extra workers, in addition to the 3 workers you start with, by passing certain spaces on the river, fame or money track. It can happen that some players have more workers than others at a certain point. The final thing we want to talk about is the end game trigger. Rajas of the Ganges is a fantastic worker placement game that gives players a lot of options and possibilities. Using dice as a resource adds some randomness that may or may not work in your favor. After you’ve played the game a couple of times you’ll get used to the end game trigger and the low scores that come with it. Definitely a game that’ll stay in our collection and will see a lot more plays.

Título: The Variants Take Rajas of the Ganges to a Whole New Level!
Contenido:
Rajas of the Ganges is a strategy game with a combination of different mechanisms and a very unique end game trigger.



Gameplay Overview

In Rajas of the Ganges the players take the roles of nobles in 16th century India, trying to build influential estates that will give them fame and wealth beyond measure!

Each player starts the game with an empty province board, a few dice and 3 workers but all of those keep improving over time. During the game players take turns placing their workers and using their available dice in order to build their province and earn fame and wealth.

The fame and wealth of each player are marked on tracks around the board, one going clockwise and the other counterclockwise. When the 2 markers meet, the end game is triggered. Some players are then allowed to take an extra turn so that everyone has taken the same amount of turns in that round, and the game ends. Whoever has the biggest cross over of the fame and wealth trackers wins the game!

There are several ways to earn wealth and fame throughout the game. The main focus is building your province and earning rewards through that but it’s definitely not the only way. In order to do that, you’ll also need to keep getting more dice and eventually a few more workers.

All of that can be accomplished through the different actions you can take in your turns, like purchasing tiles and building them on your board, travelling the river to gain benefits, visiting the palace for various different results and going to the market place to sell your goods.

Always make sure to keep an eye on everyone’s wealth and fame trackers though, because the game starts slow but in the end everything happens pretty fast!



Components

Rajas of the Ganges is a well-produced game.

Everything is of good quality, the board is double sided in order to accommodate for different numbers of players and the insert is exceptional.

The artwork is beautiful and very thematic. The board can feel a bit busy at first but honestly, that felt like it added to the theme. I don’t think anyone would characterize the Indian artwork as minimalistic and that’s why it feels right for this game board to be full of details and intricate designs! When you start paying close attention, it is also very informative and offers everything you need to know for each action and its costs and benefits.



Rules, Setup & Game Time

At first, the rules for Rajas of the Ganges felt like a lot of information and the symbols were overwhelming. It didn’t take long though to realize that at its core it’s a rather simple game and the symbols are very straight forward. As we mentioned already, you have everything you need to know marked on the board and there is no need to keep checking the rulebook after a while.

The setup is fast and the great insert helps a lot with that. The only part that takes a couple of minutes is shuffling the province tiles and stacking them appropriately but it really isn’t much at all.

The game time estimation on the box felt pretty accurate but it also depends on the mode you’ll choose to play. We noticed the game taking a bit longer with the added variants but nothing that would greatly surpass the suggested game duration. Naturally, it also depends on the players and how much time they need to decide on their action but that goes without saying.



Our Thoughts

While playing the base mode of the game, the one initially introduced in the rulebook, we felt like Rajas of the Ganges was missing something. Thankfully this feeling went away the moment we added the suggested variants! Playing the game in its simple form is probably a necessary step for a smooth teach, but we believe that it is essential to try the variants if you want the full experience of the game! After that, it quickly joined the list of our favorite games and we know we’ll be bringing it back to the table often!

Rajas of the Ganges is one of those games that are easy to grasp but at the same time offers a lot of different options to the players. Keeping your mind and eyes open to different strategies is essential and also keeps the game fresh for long!

Rajas of the Ganges is a game that includes a lot of dice rolling and apart from placing a worker, you’ll almost always be required to use some of your dice as well for the different actions. All in all, we think that Rajas of the Ganges is an excellent choice if you like worker placement, tile placement, dice rolling and a very unique end trigger! We know it’s going to stay in our collection and we would recommend you check it out if what you read sounded interesting!

Título: Silver Duck Reviews: Rajas of the Ganges
Contenido:
Plays: 4 at player counts: 2 + 3

Appearance

The front of the box shows a beautiful landscape and river with a deity holding dice and is full of colour and detail. The back of the box shows a 2 player game in progress showcasing the majority of the components and gives a description of the theme of the game. The game has a good insert which holds the components well and makes setup a lot quicker. The player boards are well laid out but a little bland until you lay tiles on them during the game. The tiles are more detailed with various spices and buildings, along with a large number and colour to denote what they require to be built. The wooden coloured player components are easy to distinguish from one another and include some shaped meeples along with some bland cubes. The main board is a particular highlight of the components, it is very colourful and detailed and doesn’t look overwhelming. Finally the dice are very good quality in bright colours with a nice weight to them.

Gameplay

The aim of the game is to make your fame and wealth track markers intersect one another, these run in parallel around the board in opposite directions. When a player’s 2 markers intersect the end of the game is triggered, with the player with the biggest difference between the 2 markers winning if multiple player’s tokens intersected. Each turn players will place workers, pay any costs associated with the spot and carry out the action listed. These costs vary from moving back on the money track to paying a specific colour or numbered dice. Players will continue to place workers until all workers have been placed and the board will then reset with players collecting back all their workers and the next round can begin as long as the end game hasn’t been triggered.

There are 4 main different worker placement areas: the quarry, market place, palace and harbour. Using the quarry worker area you can build a province tile into your personal province. There are 12 different province tiles on offer at any time, 3 for each different coloured dice. Your must pay the listed money cost when placing your worker along with matching coloured dice of equal to or greater pip value than the tile you are acquiring. You must place these tiles with a direct path back to your starting palace on your province. You gain any yields that you connect to on the edge of your province and receive the money from any markets on the tile. You also receive fame for any buildings on the tile based on your position on that buildings scroll track on the main board, so between 2 and 4 fame. There are various actions and bonuses on the board that allow you to move up on the 4 building scroll tracks.

The market worker area allows you to collect income from the markets in your province, either from the same type of market or different ones. When activating the same market you must pay a dice and can only activate as many markets as the pip value of the dice. The palace has 3 main areas the terrace, balconies and the chamber. In the terrace and balconies you can either collect a specific coloured dice of your choice or trade one coloured dice for 2 of a different coloured dice. Whenever you collect dice you roll them and place them onto your Kali statue if you have room. You can always spend karma (if you have it) to flip a dice when you need to pay a cost. In the chambers you can spend a dice of a specific number to activate one of the characters for a specific action. These include gaining dice, karma, scroll movement, taking first player and building over province tiles in your area.

In the harbour you can move your boat 1 to 3 spaces on the river and take the listed bonus on the space you end your movement on. The amount of spaces you move depends on the value of the dice spent. Boats cannot be on the same space and you simply skip spaces opponents boats are present on. The bonuses are very similar to the other actions on the board but towards the end reward dice, money or fame based on a condition such as number of karma, scroll movement or markets built in your province. You start the game with 3 workers but can unlock up to 3 more from the fame, money and river tracks when your corresponding marker passes them. Play continues until the end of the game when one player’s score markers intersect. Play continues until every player has played an even number of turns and then the winner is determined. The turns flow very naturally in this game with simple to resolve actions and costs to pay. The game plays 2 to 4 players and an unofficial solo automa is also available on bgg.

Theme and Game Length

The game has a good theme, different to many other Euro games of this weight but it doesn’t really come through in the actions you are performing. Why are you trying to get wealth and fame to meet, who are these characters and why does the river give the bonuses it does. Gaining workers makes slightly more sense as when you get more famous on the 3 tracks more people are willing to work for your province. The game doesn’t drag at all and is actually quite fast for the level of decisions offered. The game scales well with 2 different sizes of the board to tighten up the worker placement spots. The game takes about 20 minutes per player which is very good considering the weight of decisions you make.

Strategy

There are two main ways to score points in this game, wealth and fame. It is very hard to just work on just one of these and win so over the course of the game you will be earning both. Players will differ in their overall strategy but there is only the one way to win, intersecting your markers. A focus you must have is to get more workers as quickly as possible. The two workers that can be unlocked on the wealth and fame tracks are the easiest to obtain. The wealth track also offers multiple other bonuses so is the track you should target at the start of the game. If other players have more workers than you for too long you have no chance of winning with the yield tiles offering too little in compensation for the worker placement actions they are taking.

Each fame is worth 2 money so this obviously looks more appealing to try and race up but without the track bonuses this shouldn’t be an early game focus. Instead you should look to increase your scrolls for the 4 building types. If you specialise in one or two building types other players may build tiles with that building type to prevent you from building it. Also with 4 different building types the tiles that may come out may not be the ones you get extra fame for so it’s probably best to spread your scroll movement out. A space that you cannot underestimate is the space to activate up to 3 different markets in your province. You want to make sure to build 3 different high value markets as soon as possible to take advantage of this space. This spot (or spots in the 3/4 player games) is usually the first spot players go to at the start of each round from the mid game onwards. It costs no dice and can yield anywhere from 3 to 9 money which is better than any other spot due to having no costs. Activating multiple markets of the same type can also be good but requires a lot of the same market to be built which depends a lot of the tiles that come out.

Many of the spots cost dice of various values with every value having a potential good use. If you get lots of high value dice you should be using these to build tiles, trying not to waste too much pip value. The lower value dice can be used for some of the characters or more likely the river. The river is sort of split into two halves by your unlockable worker with the first half offering small bonuses like dice, scroll movement and fame. The later half offers powerful one off bonuses like activating multiple markets or gaining fame and money based on a condition. It is perfectly viable to skip most of the early bonuses and plan to maximise these powerful one off bonuses instead. With so many spots costing dice you always want to make sure you don’t run out. You should never take the terrace actions to gain one dice as it is so inefficient and should instead look to use bonuses to acquire dice or trade one dice for two. In the higher player count games you also want to make sure you are not 3rd or 4th in turn order for long due to some worker placement spots being clearly better than others and you can really fall behind if you are not careful.

There have been some runaway leader problems in the games that I have played of this. During the mid game it became obvious who was going to win generally because of the market setup they had on their board. This meant one worker could gain them 6 – 12 money with each action which was much stronger than the other players. Newer players will struggle with experienced players with both tracks to manage and not being able to distinguish which worker placement spots are better than others. The game has modest replayability as you are aiming to do the same thing each time and the way the tiles come out and what dice values you get will change each game. The game also comes with several modules which significantly help replayability and make the game better in my opinion with ways to customise your province. The game can end very suddenly and can be difficult to plan for so you need to be building towards the end of the game constantly.

Accessibility

The game is light to medium weight with simple to perform and resolve actions but some setup on your province tile required to do well. There is also some consideration needed for when and where to place your workers to maximise their benefits. The game is very easy to teach with obvious iconography and the main board layout also helps. The rulebook is excellent, very clearly laid out rules with multiple examples and text descriptions of each worker placement spot. The game takes up a decent amount of table space with each player’s province, the main board and places for tiles and dice.

Final Thoughts

Rajas of the Ganges is a good next step worker placement Euro game with simple to understand rules and great components. The game has good flow to it with your points increasing steadily before exploding towards the end. One of my main problems is the market space on the main board especially the activate 3 different ones in your province. This is way too strong due to having no cost and makes first player way too powerful in this game with players needing to shift it multiple rounds in a row to mitigate this. I really like the scoring in this game it’s fairly unique and offers different considerations unlike normal Euro style games. I also like the fact that all values of dice have uses in this game so even though low numbers aren’t good for building you can use them on characters and the river or to trade. A solid light to medium weight game.

7/10

A mini review after 11 plays, taken and from our blog Games for Two

Ganges is a dice worker placement race game. Players begin with three workers, four differently coloured dice, and markers on both a money and fame tracks which run both ways round the edge of the board. It is a race to whoever can get their money and fame markers to meet, players choose whether to generate more money or fame, though often a mix is best. The simplest spaces gain a die or swap one colour for two of a different colour. Next is the river that allows moving one to three spaces to gain a bonus, which can be anything from more money, more dice, fame or market trading. Markets gain money, while temples gain fame. Tiles need to be connected by roads back to your castle and building to the edge of the board can gain additional bonuses of money, fame or dice.

The market action allows trading at one or more of your existing markets and can be a big source of money. The first worker placed at the river, market or building is free, while later ones cost a few coins, moving you back on the money track, so getting in first each round is important. Lastly there are a set of six action spaces each corresponding to one die value, which give various benefits including more dice, the ability to cheaply overbuild an existing tile or gaining some fame and the first player marker. Up to two more workers can be gained by moving past trigger points on the fame, money and river tracks, the last of the three not being gained. Having as many workers as the other players is essential to not being left behind so you need to watch how others are progressing. The personal boards come with two sides, one simpler and the other more difficult, providing a small expansion as well as a way to cater for differing degrees of experience.

This is a good game and scales well from two to four, though is better at the higher player counts due to more interaction and less predictability, as well as more churn in the building tiles.

We have come to the conclusion race games aren’t our thing and in general they don’t work so well at two players. Usually one player gets ahead and the other finds it hard to keep up, which is not something we find fun as the player behind gets despondent and the lead player isn’t being challenged. We don’t don’t enjoy games where it is clear who as won long before the end. This wasn’t a case of one player being consistently better as the wins were fairly even. This game teetered on the edge for some time but after playing it a number of times we came to realise that it just wasn’t for us and has now been sold on.


Good Points
Components are lovely, especially the dice and colourful board
Scales well from two to four, with a double sided board to help
Something good to do with all die values, so rolling sixes does not give a huge advantage.
Combination of workers and dice is unusual and interesting

Bad points

Available buildings are fairly static in a two player game
Getting off to a slow start almost guarantees that you will lose the game.
Theme is barely present
Regularly moving small distances on the money track, often multiple times in a turn, can be quite fiddly.

In summary a good game, but not for those who dislike racing .

Inka und Markus Brand sind seit Jahren Garanten für schön gefeiltes Gameplay, dass durch abwechslungsreiche, schön verzanhnte Mechnismen glänzt. So auch im hier vorliegenden Fall von Rajas of the Ganges, bei dem wir uns ins indische Mogulreich begeben und versuchen uns als Landesherrscher zu behaupten indem wir zwischen Ansehen und Reichtum abwägen müssen. Auch dieses Spiel der Brands, soviel sei vorweg gesagt, braucht, wenn man es mit der vollen Besetzung spielen möchte wieder recht viel Tischfläche. Dafür sieht das farbenfrohe Spielmaterial auf dem Tisch absolut fantastisch aus, denn beim auspacken fallen uns sofort jede Menge bunte Würfel, Plättchen und Spielfiguren in die Hände und der Spielplan sucht seinesgleichen an Detailvielfalt. Der Ersteindruck kann erschlagen, aber wie sind die Regeln?

Worum geht es ?

Zunächst aber nochmal zum Hintergrund und Spielziel. Die Spieler verkörpern Rajas und Ranis im indischen Mogulreich, das vor Reichtum nur so strotzt. Sie stehen im Wettstreit, nein es ist sogar eine Art Wettlauf, um Reichtum und Ruhm. Um diese beiden Dinge zu erreichen bauen die Spieler ihre eignen Provinzen mit Hilfe ihrer Arbeiter zu produktiven und Ansehen bringenden Reichen aus. Rajas of the Ganges hat dabei einen recht einzigartigen Siegbedingungsmechanismus, denn um das Spielfeld laufen zwei Leisten, eine für Ruhm und eine für Reichtum. Auf einer läuft man links herum auf der anderen rechts und wenn sich die Markierungssteine beider Leisten begegnen, startet das Spielende und derjenige der sie am weitesten aneinander vorbeiziehen hat lassen, gewinnt.

Wie läuft das ab ?

Jeder Spieler bekommt zu Beginn eine ganze Menge an persönlichem Spielmaterial. Mit von der Partie sind ein eigenes Provinztableau, eine Kalistatue, an die und auf der man Würfel legen muss, sowie sechs Arbeiterfiguren, fünf Karmasteine, ein Boot, sowie je einen Geld-, Bonus- und Ruhmesmarker. In die Mitte des Tisches wird das Spielbrett gelegt, dass über verschiedene Elemente verfügt. Auf der linken Seite des Spielbretts befindet sich der Palast mit vielen verschiedenen einsetzmöglichkeiten. Über ihm findet sich die Karmaweganzeige, auf der auf Stufe eins einer der karmawürfel der Spieler gesetzt wird. Getrennt wird das Spielbrett grob in zwei Hälften durch einen Flußlauf mit vielen Feldern. Auf dem Startfeld des Flusses platzieren die Spieler ihre Boote. Die rechte Hälfte des Spielplans nehmen einige Gebäude und Entwicklungsleisten ein. Auf den Gebäuden sind wieder jede Menge Einsetzfelder und auf die leisten wird jeweils in die erste Spalte ein Karmamarker der Spieler als Anzeiger gesetzt. Die Kalistatue und drei der Arbeiter legen die Spieler direkt vor sich ab. die anderen drei Arbeiter werden auf das Spielbrett gelegt. Auf den beiden das Spielfeld umlaufenden Leisten gibt es jeweils ein Feld, auf denen ein Arbeiter abgelgt wird und auch auf dem Fluß gibt es an einer Stelle eine Brücke auf der ein Arbeiter abgelegt wird. Diese Arbeiter können im Verlauf des Spiels erst hinzugewonnen werden. Zum Schluß bekommt jeder Spieler einen Würfel in jeder der vier Farben und würfelt mit ihnen. Dann werden sie an die Statuen angelegt und derjenige mit dem insgesamt geringsten Gesamtergebnis wird Startspieler. Alle anderen Würfel werden bereitgelegt.

Als letzte Vorbereitung werden noch die Plättchen nach Rückseiten sortiert. Zunächst gibt es auch diese einmal in vier verschiedenen Farben. Nach der Farbsortierung werden sie noch nach den Rückseitensymbolen getrennt. Die Rückseiten zeigen entweder eine Schlage, eine Kuh oder einen Tiger. So ergibt sich eine Auslage aus zwölf Stapeln, die neben das Spielbrett gelegt werden. Eine Menge an Vorbereitung und eine Menge an Material das nun auf dem Tisch liegt.

Der Spielverlauf ist dann aber erfreulich einfach und glänzt nur durch eine hohe Komplexität der Möglichkeiten. Im Grunde ist Rajas of the Ganges ein Worker-Placement-Spiel, in dem zusätzlich Würfel als eine Art Zahlungsmittel eingesetzt werden. Eine Runde besteht im Grunde nur aus dem Einsetzen aller Arbeiter der Spieler. Danach bekommen alle ihre Arbeiter zurück und die nächste Runde beginnt. Das passiert solange bis oben bereits beschriebenen Spielendebedingung eintrifft.

Es gibt vier Bereiche in denen die Arbeiter eingesetzt werden können: den Palast, den Steinbruch, den Marktplatz und den Hafen. Die letzten drei sind alle neben den Gebäuden auf dem Spielplan angesiedelt. Im Steinbruch kann man Bauaktionen erlangen, um sein eigenes Provinztableau zu erweitern. Auf dem Marktplatz kann man vor allem im späteren Spielverlauf Geld verdienen. Der Palast dient zweierlei Dingen, zum einen kann man hier wichtige Personen treffen und durch sie Vergünstigungen erhalten und zum anderen kann man hier Würfel bekommen und tauschen. Maximal 10 Würfel darf man an seiner Kalistatue anlegen, die Farben sind dabei egal und die Werte ebenso. Wann immer man einen neuen Würfel erhält, würfelt man ihn und bekommt ihn mit der angezeigten Augenzahl in seinen Vorrat. Im Hafen zu guter letzt kann man Arbeiter einsetzen, um das eigene Schiff auf dem Fluß vorwärts zu bewegen und Boni zu erhalten.

Weiter ins Detail bei den Aktionen werde ich erstmal nicht gehen nur punktuell, um etwas zu verdeutlichen. Die Felder auf dem Spielfeld gibt es in verschiedenen Varianten. Manchmal sind sie leer, dann ist die entsprechende Aktion kostenlos, manchmal kostet sie den Einsatz eines bestimmten Würfels oder Gold. Die Bauaktionen im Steinbruch kosten je nach Feld ein bis vier Gold für das einsetzen des Arbeiters. Im Anschluß darf man sich eines der ausliegenden Plättchen nehmen, wenn man seine Kosten auch noch bezahlen kann, denn auf den Plättchen ist immer eine Würfelfarbe mit einem Wert angegeben, der bezahlt werden muss. Zum Beispiel bedeutet ein blauer Würfel mit einer sieben, dass man insgesamt Würfel mit sovielen Augen mindestens abgeben muss. Zahlt man mehr, so bekommt man aber auch nichts zurück. Die Plättchen dienen dann unserem Provinzausbau. Unser Provinztableau zeigt am Rand überall Boni, die wir erreichen können, wenn wir sie korrekt mit Straßen anschließen. Ansonsten sind auf den Plättchen mehrere Gebäude abgibildet für die wir Ruhmespunkte oder Gold bekommen. Die Ruhmespunkte richten sich dabei nach unseren Gebäudeleisten, auf denen wir am Anfang die Marker platziert haben. Je besser hier der Ausbau, desto mehr Punkte.

Im Markt bekommt man Geld für Marktplätze in der Provinz. Dabei ist es wichtig, welche Märkte mit welchen Waren man werten möchte, denn es gibt Seide, Gewürze und Tee als Waren und die jeweiligen Märkte lassen sich über die Arbeiterfelder auch auswählen. Hier generiert man das Gold für die Goldleiste.

Der Palast ermöglicht es auf vielen Feldern Würfel einer gewünschten Farbe zu bekommen, oder einen Würfel einer Farbe gegen zwei Würfel einer anderen Farbe einzutauschen. Darüber hinaus gibt es noch sechs Felder, die sogenannten Gemächer, mit Personen, die man aufsuchen kann und bei denen man besondere Vergünstigungen erhält. Als Beispiel sei hier der Großmogul genannt, bei dem man einen Würfel mit der Augenzahl von eins abgeben muss und dafür zwei Ruhmespunkte und den Startspielerstein für die nächste Runde erhält.

Zum Schluss sei noch der Hafen erwähnt, indem man für 0-2 Gold einen Arbeiter einsetzen kann und anschließend sein Boot um ein bis drei Felder auf dem Fluß vorwärts ziehen darf. Man erhält dann die belohnung des Feldes auf dem man zum stehen kommt. Wichtig dabei ist noch, dass Felder auf denen anderen Boote stehen nicht mitzählen und man sie überspringt.

Ziel ist es nun durch all diese miteinander verzahnten Aktionen, die beiden Leisten möglichst schnell aneinander vorbei zu bewewgen, um Rajas of the Ganges zu gewinnen. Die Komplexität eerhält das Spiel dabei wirklich aus der Vielzahl an Möglichkeiten und wie sie miteinander funktionieren. Deshalb würde ich es als Kennerspiel einordnen, aber auch nicht mehr. Es ist dabei nicht ganz so strategisch wie zum Beispiel ein Teotihuacan, bei dem man noch ein wenig mehr vorrausplanen muss, was man tut. Das schadet hier zwar auch nicht, aber es gibt immer genug Möglichkeiten weiter voranzukommen.

Das Fazit

Was mir an Rajas of the Ganges wirklich wahnsinnig gut gefällt, ist die Einfachheit des eigentlichen Spielmechanismusses, den man natürlich schon hunderte Male genau so gesehen hat: setze einen Arbeiter auf ein Feld und bekomme eine Gegenleistung dafür. Mehr ist es im Prinzip ja nicht. Die Besonderheiten bei Rajas of The Ganges rühren zum einen von den Würfeln her, die hier als Ressource gebraucht werden, um bestimmte Aktionen bezahlen zu können. Dabei spielen sowohl Farbe als auch Augenzahl eine Rolle und das ist eine recht clevere Art für eine variable Ressource in einem solchen Spiel. Zum Zweiten von der wirklich weitreichenden Verzahnung der einzelnen Elemente: wir benötigen einen Arbeiter für eine bestimmte Aktion, wir benötigen einen bestimmten Würfel zur Bezahlung, wir benötigen Plättchen für den Ausbau mit bestimmten Gebäuden, wir benötigen Gebäude für Ruhm und Gold, wir benötigen beides um schnell voran zu kommen. Phänomenal überlegt.

Man hat soviele Möglichkeiten in Rajas of the Ganges zum Ziel zu kommen. Der eine setzt auf die Boni auf dem Fluß, der andere auf einen schnellen Ausbau seiner Provinz und der nächste versucht seine Leisten direkt nach vorne zu bringen. Die Brands haben hier ein wirklich gutes Spiel abgeliefert. Ein Spiel bei dem man immer gerne mitspielt. Es ist nicht das ganz große Highlight, warum kann ich gar nicht genau sagen, vielleicht weil der eigentliche Mechanismus dann doch eher simpel ist. Aber das Spielgefühl das Rajas of the Ganges vermittelt ist einfach toll. Eigentlich hätte es ein Spitzenkandidat für die Kennerspiel des Jahres Nominierung in seinem Jahrgang sein müssen, war es aber irgendwie nicht. Hier kann ein jeder Freund von Worker-Placement-Spielen bedenkenlos zugreifen und wird seine Freude damit haben. Tolles Material, tolles Spielgefühl, tolle Mechanismen, einfach ein tolles Spiel!

Verlag: Huch!
Autor(en): Inka Brand, Markus Brand
Erscheinungsjahr: 2017
Spieleranzahl: 2 – 4
Dauer: 45 – 75 Minuten

Rajas of the Ganges shares similarities with Castles of Burgundy, but I felt that game a bit dry. And Voyages of Marco Polo as well, but there I found the travelling really painful, and difficult to trade offs against other choices.

RoG has the exciting parts of CoB, i.e. the dice placement, with some mitigants (karma) for bad die rolls.

But it also has several other parts which seem a bit random, but really well balanced, i.e.
- the tile placement, which rewards you with fame (VP) for buildings, money, for markets, but above all, requires some balance so that you leave space for further tiles (that can even yield bonuses if they are well placed)
- the engine building, i.e. markets allow you to score a lot of revenues repeatedly, if you manage to build several markets of the same kind of good (which is constrained both by the availability of such tiles as well as your ability to connect them to previous tiles). Buildings can score a lot of fame, especially if you focus on one type and manage to upgrade the corresponding track for that building.
- the intermediary bonuses (that encourage you to move up on the fame or money track, instead of only building an engine) whether you progress in fame, in money, or on the river, rewarding you with money, workers, building your revenue per building, or advancing on the river.
- the most exciting of all, the idea that you can win using fame, or money (through trading), or any combination - and that there are very valid strategies in either combination.

It may seem that all these make a mishmash of mechanisms, but I found it is far from overwhelming, and felt much more intuitive than other games which have a bit too many different things happening (e.g. Tzolkin, Gaia Project)

From the 'worker placement' perspective, the board has a pretty good balance of scarcity of some spots (i.e. only one worker per round) vs. availability of others (but with some charge to pay for not being the first one), making it much less constraining than some WP games where you need to think 3 turns ahead and hope nobody is going to ruin your plans.

And unlike WP games like Lords of Waterdeep (which is obviously a much lighter game), you can have better use of your workers if you avoid using the spots that others are going for (while in LoW, it's pretty random, depending on the cubes they need for their quests, so really hard to see a pattern). This increases (non-random) player interaction.

Overall, I was very pleased by my first game, and am looking forward to play it again!

Título: Fame or Fortune
Contenido:
Fame. How many times have we dreamt of becoming a celebrity? To be an all-star athlete, an actor or a prestigious musician? We may not envy celebrities’ lack of intimacy, but we may lust their comfortable lifestyles. The toll to pay for fame seems to come at a high price though. Regardless of the preference, fame or wealth in extreme doses can lead to unstable lifestyles. Just like many things in life, balanced is needed. Same applies to this game, in which players take roles as Rajas; racing each other to become the wealthiest or most renowned. Whatever path you choose though, there can only be one ruler. Let the race being and let’s find out who will be the next rising power in this review for Rajas of the Ganges.


Rajas of the Ganges, another Euro Game from the successful Brand’s, made it into my top games released in 2017. This is a race game, so those of you that like games with a numerical scoring system may not enjoy. There can only be one (no, this is not highlander), and there won’t be second breakfast or third luncheon. However, don’t let this be a reason to scare you off. This game is simply fantastic! I was too, skeptical to purchase it. Although a fan of the Brand’s, I’m not too keen on race games. What got me intrigued though, was “worker placement” + the racing element. That combination made it different than anything else I played. The beauty of the game comes in that merge of the beautiful aspects of worker placement, the tactical challenge of efficient action-selection; to either have the upper hand in resources or by advancing faster in any of the two tracks than your opponents. But before I go into much more detail of why I’m enamored with this game, let me give more insight of the game itself and how it works.


The game is set in India at the time of the rise of the Mogul Empire. The rulebook comes with some informative content about that era and terminology. If you are familiar with the Brand’s, you know that their bigger Euros come with many choices in regards to action selection. This game is definitely no exception. At first, the board looks overwhelming, but once you understand the concept and what you’re aiming for, you’ll be surprised with the swift pace and lack of AP. The objective of the game is to be the most successful Raja. To achieve this, there are two tracks on the board: one for Fame (moves clockwise) and one for wealth (counter clock). During the game, you will take actions that advance your marker in one of the two tracks until they meet each other. At that point, every other player between that player and the starting player can take one more action to attempt to catch up, afterwards the game ends. Whoever is able to connect both of their tracks ties for the win; the Tie breaker being the player with the farthest distance in which one track has super passed the other.
I won’t go into great detail of the game or actions, but allow me a brief description. First, the actions you choose help in advancing your markers from one of the two tracks (or both). Other actions are useful to collect more resources (dice) or give you other bonuses. Gathering dice is crucial, as several actions require sacrificing one or more dice. There are 4 main places you can visit. The Ganges River, the Market, the Palace and the Quarry. The Ganges is set with multiple spaces along the river, and each player owns a boat for navigation. To do so, you place a worker in an empty space, pay cost (if any), and sacrifice a dice with a value of 1-3 to move that many spaces up the river (boats can only move forward and land in unoccupied slots). Moving through the river is useful as it grants immediate bonuses, such as dice, building upgrades, prestige, etc. Next, there is the palace, which has the most available spaces. There, you place workers that require no dice or dice-dependent actions that are more powerful, and grant bonuses including karma, upgrades, first player token among other things. The market and quarry are crucial to advance both the wealth and fame markers. When you visit the quarry, you have the option to buy tiles to allocate into your personal player board and gain bonuses. Those bonuses include market bonuses (money) or building bonuses (to advance on the prestige track). Some tiles have a combination of both. The market on the other hand, allows activating specific markets that you have built on your board to increase wealth and further advance on that track. Collecting money matters. Not only is it necessary to advance in the wealth track, but several actions require a monetary expense, besides dice.


The setup of the game is simple. Each player in the game starts with a Kali Goddess Board to place your dice (8-10 max), a dice of each color and a player board with your palace where you will build the tiles purchased from the quarry. Each round, players take turns placing workers one at a time to perform any available action until no player has workers left and the round ends. The workers are collected back, the first player marker is given to the appropriate player and you do the same thing again all over until someone reaches the victory condition. Simple, right? Let’s get into what makes this game as good as sliced bread


The game flow of this game is simply phenomenal. For me, it’s addicting as chocolate. Turns go faster than Nascar (perhaps not a good analogy). In a two-player game I almost feel like I’m playing with a chess clock at lightning speed just like Josh and Vinnie would play chess in Searching for Bobby Fischer. Even at higher player counts, I believe it hasn’t been longer than 1 h 15 min. But it’s not only the pace what makes it so fun. The game is very tactical; the most efficient players end up wining and among experienced players the finale is always close. Actions are exclusive, so predicting opponent’s strategy matters. Blocking does not feel cutthroat; there are too many actions in the board to be fully blocked. The game is interactive as it can be crucial in final rounds to stop powerful moves from opponents that could end the game. The actions are simple and easy to understand. This allows for the game to operate elegantly and flow smoothly like a good bourbon in your mouth. I also like how the game evolves after every round. As you advance in either of the two tracks, or on the Ganges, you will eventually claim an extra worker and take more actions per turn. The urge to get those workers makes you strategize and possibly change gears every turn. I can’t highlight enough how much I like the pace and thrill of this game!


One other aspect that I really enjoy about the game is the lack of dependence on “good” rolls. The game is extremely balanced in that sense. Some actions require high numbers, others low. In that context, high numbers are needed to buy valuable tiles, while low rolls let you navigate the Ganges or go to certain spots at the palace. It doesn’t stop there though. Some actions allow exchanges of different colored dice. There is also this thing called karma. Karma (up to 3) lets you flip a dice towards its opposite side. Overall, all this allows for more versatility and maneuverability towards dice manipulation. For a game in which dice are so crucial, to have multiple ways to manipulate them is utterly important.


The production design is stellar, thematic and the board easy to set up. There is little downtime setting up the game and storage is quite easy. The variability in this game is high. The player boards are double sided and you can play the game in basic or advanced mode. I recommend playing the basic mode for a couple of times to become familiar with the boards. Also, on your player board, some areas have additional bonuses that are claimed if a road on a tile is connected to it. Playing in basic mode allows for all players to have same bonuses before you get into the advanced game with variable set ups. In addition, there are 9 Ganges tiles to randomly distribute throughout the river if variable setup is desired.


Are there any drawbacks? If I were to mention some “negatives”; 1) the board for the 2-player side is slightly less vivid than the back; 2) the first player token (elephant token!), is fiddly and the legs tend to fall. Regarding gameplay; I have read online comments about the game starting to feel “samey”. Others say that some actions are broken. The only action that I will agree is consistently abused is the second slot on the palace. Especially in the basic mode. It’s almost a must to go there ASAP and get the immediate bonus; getting 3 dice from this action is the best return you’ll get. In advanced mode it’s slightly less powerful, as bonuses have to be allocated on player boards to later be activated throughout tiles. Complains also arise in regards to strategy. People claim the wealth route as the way to go. Granted, you can focus on one commodity market and advance very fast on the wealth track. That track advances twice as fast as the fame so it moves fairly quickly. However, it’s up to the opponent to maneuver and stop your strategy from being that efficient. I have lost games where I had the better wealth engine. I think that this game is more about evolving in a short to medium term basis, depending on how your opponents are playing. One possible drawback is that the game can be cruel to newbies. An experienced player will have advantage. I’ve also read complains of the game being unrewarding and of circumstances where the winner is settled way before the game ends. Similar to the criticism for Lewis & Clark, where people mention that there are games in which even 30 min in advance the winner is already determined. I have honestly not experienced that scenario with Rajas. Overview:

Power is where fame and wealth intersect. In "Rajas of the Ganges" [Rajas] you'll play the part of one of the minor nobles along the Ganges river seeking your fortune. By sending your representatives to build your estates, hobnob at the high mogul's palace, and trade on the Ganges River, you'll race to be the first to cement your power. Hardware:

Rajas has mechanics present in other games, but what makes it unusual is the combination of mechanics present. This is reflected in the game's unusual collection of bits, including 48 dice (12 each of different colors). The meeples are pretty standard wooden fare but the art printed on the various boards helps to fuel the theme.

Gameplay:

It would have been easy for Rajas to degenerate into a much-maligned point salad of simple worker placement. Indeed, there are probably some players out there who would argue that's what it really is anyway. There were two things about Rajas that for me that pulled it ahead of the just-another-sad-Euro crowd: dice manipulation, and the scoring tracks.

Certain actions taken through Rajas will require dice showing certain numbers or dice of certain colors. It's not quite currency as there is a "money" track, but thematically I think of the dice as the ups-and-downs involved in the pursuit of fortune and glory. Among the actions you can take are turning in individual dice for two dice of a different color, or placing workers to gain individual dice if you're out of options and running out of dice. Running out of dice will severely handicap you so it always pays to think ahead as far as you can.

What's that you say? Dice hate you? In Rajas, having low dice is an advantage if you want to use them to sail the Ganges. And if you need a 6 when you rolled a 1, the "Karma" mechanic allows you to flip a die to its opposite side as you go to pay for an action. All in all, Rajas is far more skill based than the presence of dice might suggest.

Two tracks surrounding the board represent wealth, which is fairly mercurial but easier to get (and must be spent on certain actions along the way), and fame, which is not as easy to get but can not be lost once it is acquired. The ultimate goal is to make the tracks pass each other as they move in opposite directions.

The goal is simple but as you may have already figured out, the gameplay is not. Besides sending representatives to the high mogul's palace you will also be building your own estate on a separate board with an eye towards the wealth and fame that those buildings can bring.

Pros:

The dice and scoring tracks make for a rather unusual, if not unique, game overall.

If you're a fan of worker placment-style Euros, you'll probably slide right into Rajas quite comfortably.

Rajas does not rely heavily on text outside of the rulebook. That can help when trying to teach someone the game who isn't fluent in English.

Cons:

There can be something of a point-salad feel.

The limited number of actions you can take out of all the available actions in a turn can lead to some pretty severe analysis paralysis.

There can be a sense that the game is decided quite early when one player breaks out far ahead of the others. This tends to be less of a problem in 3-4 player games, though it can happen in any player count.

Final Thoughts:

Rajas of the Ganges is in some ways a natural evolution of the worker placement genre. It adds a layer of complexity when compared to some older worker placement games which I happen to like. It's now available for play at Yucata.de and I definitely recommend giving it a try.

each of the 3 tracks (money, fame, and river) offer a myriad of bonuses for certain points along the track (or every space in the case of the river) from improving buildings, fame, money, dice, karma (which is spent to flip dice over), bonus tiles, and extra workers.

I like that the luck associated with dice rolling is mitigated somewhat by the ability to use karma to flip a die.

I also like the little bonuses for placing tiles around the estate and advancing along the money and fame tracks. Those elements provide some direction in the early game.

The money track advances at half the rate of the fame track, but money is much, much easier to get than fame. And that's the problem.

Everyone spends the first couple of turns buying tiles with decent markets, even if the tiles have roads that would prevent a player from taking advantage of the bonuses long the side of the estate board. Little else in this game matters compared with getting those markets down early. After a player has some good markets in their estate, they use the market space with a worker or worker and die to generate money, usually around $7 to $12 depending on the market being used.

The second thing players work on is advancing the fame track by placing just enough tiles with buildings to get their fame token far enough up the track to unlock another worker. The fame track goes up as high as 60, but it's rare to have a player go past 25. The last game I played, the winner had only advanced on the fame track to 16.

One might wonder why it's faster to advance on the money track if the fame track is 1/2 as long. The primary way to move along the fame track is to build tiles with buildings. This costs money and a die each time. When the tile is placed, the player gets a one-time advancement on the fame track anywhere from 2 to 4 points per building on the tile, depending on advancements levels. So unlike markets, these buildings will never generate any more points. On the other hand, tiles with markets provide a money bonus when built that is usually equal to or more than the money cost to build it, and they can be triggered at each round in the future, sometimes more than once.

After the first three or four turns, the game devolves into a battle to see who can activate the market spaces, with players spending any remaining actions, on minor actions to get more dice or a few minor advancements here and there.

As a result every game pretty much plays out the same way. Yes, there are a lot of choices to make in Rajas of the Ganjes, but the power of the markets dramatically outweigh all the other options, especially the mixed use market that a player can use without even having to use up a die. This makes for a very unsatisfying mid and late-game turns.

Rajas of the Ganjes is a good game, but it's lack of well-balanced choices make each play feel just like the one before it and prevents it from being a great game. I'd be interested to hear if anyone has developed a house-rule or two to fine-tune this game. As it stands, if someone were to ask me to play a worker placement game with a bit of randomness thrown in, I'd pull Orléans or Feast for Odin off the shelf before I'd put Rajas of the Ganges back on the table.

Título: Worker and dice placement in a colourful setting
Contenido:
Designed by Inka Brand and Markus Brand, and published by Huch!, this is a colourful thematically atmospheric game that evokes the pre-Colonial India of the Moguls. In Rajas of the Ganges, each player is a raja or rani developing their estates and provinces as they seek to increase their wealth and fame.

Asian setting notwithstanding, this is a light euro game. It’s a worker placement game where players are sending their workers to different locations to take the indicated actions. Players each start initially with three workers but they can add up to two more as they advance along the scoring tracks and/or the Ganges river.

As you can see from the 360 degree photo on Board's Eye View (www.facebook.com/boardseye), Rajas of the Ganges is a game that looks good on the table, with attractive components that help to draw players in. Though there is a lot going on, it is nonetheless easy to teach and learn, thanks in no small part to the very clear iconography for all the of various actions on offer. The game plays equally well with 2 or 4 (the board is double-sided so that the two-player game is played on a subtly different board) and you can expect a game to run for just over an hour. The game comes with a couple of mini-expansions that vary the play by introducing additional options. They can also be used as a way of handicapping experienced players when they are competing with those new to the game.

Players are scoring their fame and wealth, each of which tracks around the board in the opposite direction. The winner is the first to get his fame and wealth to intersect. This mechanic helps to broaden the strategies that each player can choose to take: it is possible to win, for example, by focusing heavily on fame and barely making any net progress on the wealth track, and vice versa. It makes the game feel less like a "points salad".

There are occasions when wealth has to be spent (paying to place a worker out at a particular location) but the main ‘currency’ in the game is the dice. Most locations demand that the player spends a die to place a worker there. Some locations demand a die of a specific colour but showing any number, others a die with a specific number but of any colour. Players start off with one die of each of the four colours and, since dice are spent (lost to the player) every time they are used to place out a worker, one of the key actions players have to balance is the need to visit locations that will give them more dice. Every time a die is acquired it is rolled, permanently setting its numerical value.

If they are not careful, players risk running out of dice, but there is otherwise no shortage of desirable locations to visit. That includes locations that will usually require spending two or three dice of the same colour to choose a building tile to lay out in the player’s province (their individual board). The buildings on tiles mostly earn fame points, though players can increase wealth by building markets.

If you perennially complain that dice hate you because you always roll low, then this is a game that offers you some recompense. Though players will benefit more from high rolls when buying tiles, the Ganges river offers generous opportunities for earning bonuses and workers can only be placed to move along the river with die values of 1, 2 or 3. And there is always the option of spending ‘karma’ to flip a die to its opposite side (for example, turning a 1 into a 6). Karma is another element that can be earned as a bonus during the course of the game.

In play, Rajas of the Ganges is a fairly gentle game. This is a game where each player is forging their own individual path to victory. There will be occasions when an opponent takes a tile you wanted to get, or blocks you by placing a worker out at the very location you were hoping to go to, but that’s pretty much the full extent of the interaction. This is not a ‘take that’ game where you are attacking or deliberately sabotaging each other. For some that will be a negative but for many it’s a huge plus. This is a game you can enjoy with friends and family members without any risk of falling out.

And it’s a very satisfying experience. As you increasingly build up your province by acquiring and laying out tiles, so your progress along the victory tracks accelerates, making for an exciting climax. Definitely one to check out.

This review first appeared in Episode 27 of The Five By Podcast. This is a transcript of the podcast segment that begins at minute mark 10:45.

Money? Or Points?
So many of these games we love ask us to choose:
Do we slowly get points all game long?
Do we get lots of money, using our purchasing power to get lots of points late?
When do we pivot from our pursuit of money to acquire the winning horde of VICTORY POINTS?
2017’s Rajas of the Ganges, designed by famed designers Inka  and  , doesn’t ask you to compromise between money and points. It asks you to see yourself as a great noble in 16th century India along the Ganges river, racing to leverage your personal empire to acquire great wealth, or great prestige, or some measure of both.
The gameboard is framed by not one, but two, tracks: one for Rupees, and one for prestige. These two tracks start in the same corner but run around the board in opposite directions. Player markers run on each track, and the winner is the person who can get their two markers to meet first. Prestige is harder to come by, but moves quickly around the board once scored. Money can be gained or spent, in the service of your goals. Both tracks give you small but important tools as you meet certain thresholds, like new workers and more dice.
In service of these goals you have a unique hybrid worker and dice placement system. There are ample supplies of 4 colors of d6 in the game, and you start with one of each. Dice are always rolled when you receive them. On your turn you will place one of your workers alongside a die on one of many locations on the board.
In the palace you can use dice of a certain number or color to gain dice or take special actions that propel your game in asymmetric directions. At the docks, you can use small valued dice of any color to travel along the Ganges, each space offering unique and helpful rewards. At the quarry you use dice of specific colors and number thresholds to purchase estate tiles. These tiles help complete your own personal estate earning a wide variety of rewards and helpful tools along the way. Each tile has either one of 4 building types on it, and as you acquire these tiles you gain prestige for how renowned you are with this building type. Some tiles have craftsmen on them that help you earn money when you visit the last major area of the game board: the market.
All of this is a race. As you alternate taking short, efficient turns, you can see each player’s two markers inching closer and closer to each other. Along the way you attempt to get into a flow, the kind of state where if you time it right, actions ripple, generating addictive combos and rewards that allow you efficient success in the turns to come, and if you don’t, you spend turns simply getting dice so that you can get going again.
Now I tend to eschew race games, preferring not to see the win as a foregone conclusion with a ¼ of the game left. But, Rajas of the Ganges does not have that problem. In my 14 plays so far, the last round has always been tense and thrilling, with close finishes and unexpected blowouts. We have won by getting rich. We have won by acquiring prestige. And we have won by equally chasing both. Each of those paths to victory can be pursued from multiple directions. The race is always so much fun.
I do have two complaints with the game which are directed squarely at the Publisher   These issues were easy fixes during development, and someone should have raised a warning flag or two.
First, I can’t believe that there wasn’t more thought and research put into which Hindu God would be depicted in the game. With literally millions of Hindu Gods to choose from, the depiction of Kali as a dice wielding peaceful skull-less tongue-less goddess was head-scratchingly thoughtless at best. At worst, it is cultural appropriation without respect for the religion. When you choose a theme based on a particular time and place you have an obligation to pay some respect to that place and time and get it right. Better options include: Setting the game in a fantasy or futuristic setting instead of a known time and place, or at the very least being super vague about the goddess and not naming her specifically in the rulebook.
Second, I am not color blind, but this game makes me question the validity of that statement. This game is ridiculously difficult for me to distinguish between the green and blue dice. I can’t believe that there isn’t yet a thread about this on BGG. We had to build a foam core insert for the tiles and dice and label them on the side so that I wouldn’t mix up the dice. I wish they had found some ways to correct for this difficulty, like a specific area on your player board for dice of specific colors, I know that there are many possible solutions that would make this game possible for more people to play.
Regardless of these shortcomings, this game is a joy to play, and Inka  and   unsurprisingly have built a winner yet again.
Happy gaming all! This is Kathryn Harmon and you can find me at katlibrary on BGG, or @kibrarian on Twitter both with a k.

Having high expectations can either leave you disappointed when the gaming experience doesn’t meet them or create this kind of weird psychological situation where instead of just playing and enjoying the game for the sake of it, you’re playing in order to convince yourself that the game is as enjoyable as you want it to be. Does that make sense?

J: Kind of?

M: Anyway, I had heard a bit about this game beforehand, but didn’t really have any expectations coming in.


COMPONENTS

J: As I said in the intro the artist of this game is incredible so all of the illustrations are gorgeous. The board is just a colourful, lush feast for the eyes. Even the art on the back of the province tiles is exquisite.

M: That board is a tad busy though. There is a lot of iconography smushed into a small area that I think takes away from the overall look and makes it a bit hard to look at for the first little while until you get used to it.

J: There’s also the thematically shaped worker meeples, little wooden boats and a 3D first player marker… of a tiny man riding an elephant!

M: I’m not going to lie, when I become first player I think I’m just as excited to grab that elephant as I am to get first choice of action. But I want to talk about the dice.

J: The dice! Beautiful, vibrant, translucent clickity-clackety resources that fit nicely into the hands of both me and my beautifully-illustrated personal dice holder.

M: I was actually going to say that I can’t tell the difference between the blue and the green. I can’t tell you the number of times I went to grab a blue die and ended up with a green one instead. Or vice-versa. Surely they could have used a different shade of one or the other. Between examining the dice and looking at the board, I think I spent more time squinting playing this game than I do when I forget my glasses at a fast food order counter.

J: It’s not that hard to see the difference, especially when they’re beside each other, which they usually are.

M: I’m just saying, if you have problems distinguishing between colours, that might be something to take into consideration.



THEME

M: The theme is… uh… the theme is that you are…uh… James what’s the theme?

J: In this game, players take on the roles of rajas and ranis in India during the era of the Mogul empire. You must build the most fabulous province with grandiose structures and trading markets to become the nation’s most legendary leader.

M: Ah right.

J: The theme may not come through as strongly as in other games, but it does still add to the experience. You get to move your boat down the river, you see your wealth and fame grow with your province that you’re building in front of you, and you genuinely do feel like you are becoming more rich and powerful as the game goes.

M: Yes, you are whisked away to a magical land where you trade giant, colourful dice for land and after visiting a dancer, you leave with “gifts” that you actually want.


GAMEPLAY

J: Turns in this game are lightning fast.

M: It doesn’t take long until it comes around to your turn again. Each time you’re kind of taking a small step towards that turn when you do something awesome.

J: It’s kind of like walking up a staircase with a bunch of landings and on each landing is some treasure. Take 3 or 4 steps where you don’t really get anything, but you’re inching closer toward that next great prize. When you get it, it feels great, but then you immediately need to start figuring out how to get to the next one. Setting yourself up to collect multiple bonuses for a huge pay day is super satisfying.

M: Not the best analogy, but I guess? Anyway, what did you think of the converging money and fame tracks?

J: Loved it! As a victory condition, this really works. Unlike in some other race type games, you can easily see where everyone stands at all times by taking a quick glance at the board. And the game really ramps up at the end, so in the beginning of the game you’re making these tiny incremental movements where you’re not really getting anywhere on the tracks, but then before you know it, you’re sprinting to the finish. So even if the track shows that someone is far behind, you never really feel like they’re completely out of it.

M: Of course there will be games when someone is so far behind that they clearly have no shot though, which can be a pretty helpless feeling and make the last few turns a drag.

J: Speaking of feeling, I love the balance of serenity and tension that exists in this game. First, the game feels nice and peaceful as you handle the pretty dice, float your boat down the river and place your province tiles. Then as the game progresses so does the tension as you see your opponents’ money and fame markers inching closer together. Then you feel like you’re watching an intense episode of (insert intense drama title here) and you find yourself watching every single move, holding your breath thinking things like “please don’t take the dancer”, “don’t you dare activate those markets” and “if they take that tile I’m screwed”.

M: That can be annoying though when someone ruins your perfect plan and you have to re-think everything to settle for a less optimal plan. Another thing that can be annoying in dice games in general is when you keep getting bad rolls.

J: Being able to change your plans is part of the fun though. It you want to be able to get whatever you want, whenever you want without anyone getting in the way then maybe you should play solo. The way dice are used in this game is actually really cool. Mostly, they’re resources that are used to purchase things such as tiles and other favours. Having bad rolls isn’t really an issue because low numbers are desirable almost as often as high numbers. You can also use karma to flip a dice over. So when you roll a 1, you can always flip it over to a 6 if you’re looking to purchase tiles. I really didn’t find the old “luck of the dice” problem to be an issue at all in this game.

M: Okay, last thing. I’m a little concerned that going after the money track is much more effective than going after fame. I feel like I’ve found a strategy that works, and if I want to win, I don’t think I’ll deviate from that. That’s not a lot of variability.

J: I’ve seen other people wonder the same thing, but it’s just not true. I don’t want to say too much about strategy because I want people who haven’t played yet to be able to discover it on their own. However, I have won this game using different strategies and have seen the winning player’s markers meet spaces away from the beginning of the money track as well as spaces from the start of the fame track. There are also some variants included in the game which add a bit of replayability and a way for experienced players to level the playing field a bit while playing with new players.


SCALING

J: I had fun playing this at all player counts. Two is a bit more open in terms of getting tiles and bonuses that you want, whereas with more players you feel a bit more constricted and the race aspect becomes more exciting.

M: I think I enjoyed it the most with two. The turns are fast though, so even with four players the game moves pretty quickly.


FINAL THOUGHTS

J: A lot of people will point to the victory condition and use of dice as reasons why this game is elevated above being “just another euro”. I want to say something new and fresh here, but they’re right. What makes this game enjoyable for me is the satisfaction that comes from using your dice and tiles in a clever way and the tension that exists mostly because of the two victory tracks. Another hit from an excellent design team.

M: I enjoyed my plays for most of the reasons that you said, but I don’t think this is one that I’ll purchase. It’s definitely a good game, but there are SO MANY fantastic games out there. If I’m going to spend money and take up precious shelf space, a game has to just about blow me away or be my favourite of a specific niche to make it into my collection. This one just falls short for me I’m afraid.

Título: Sailing through India
Contenido:
In my reviews I concentrate on two aspects of the game. A look at what you actually get in the box. The components of the game, and a look at both the quantity and quality.

Secondly, my experiences with the game including what I like about it and anything I don’t like about it.

This time I am going to be looking at Rajas of the Ganges. It's a game designed by Markus and Inka Brand and published by Hoch. They have previously designed a lot of games both for adults and children including Village, Murano and Word Slam.

It's a game for 2 to 4 players with a play time of around 90 minutes with four players. (Listed time is 45 to 75 minutes, I think for most it will be a bit longer)

The game is a worker placement game with dice.




The box itself is a typical square box used by most games companies. It has a very colourful image on the box showing a scene of the Ganges as it meanders through India. It certainly evokes the theme of the game and is quite an attractive looking box.

So what is in the box?

The rulebook is 24 pages long, but it’s not a complicated game. The rules have a lot of illustrations and examples which help greatly in getting into the game. The last couple of pages have rules for an advanced version of the game. I have found the rules to be very good with no major rules question not answered. I looked up a couple of things the first play or two, but generally quite an easy game to learn.




Individual player boards have been with us for quite a while now, but I don’t remember a year like this where nearly all the big releases have them. As you can see Rajas is no exception.

The board represents your own personal estate, with your palace in the centre at the top of the board. Players will be purchasing and placing tiles that contain roads and buildings onto the board to represent their growing estate and influence.

Around the board you will see a number of icons on the edge of the play area, these are special yields. These represent bonuses you receive when you link to that board edge a road that links back to your palace. They are things like, money, fame, dice etc. I will explain these in more detail when we look at the main board.

A nice touch is that these boards are double sided, with the rewards on one side not being as good as the other side. This is designed so that players with less experience with the game can play with the better side, whilst more experienced players can use the weaker side.




This is some examples of the tiles that players will be building on their player boards. They come in four different colours, one for each colour of dice. Within each colour there are three different sets of tiles, differentiated by the animals on their backs. The tiles have a number in a coloured background in the top left corner. This is the amount (or more) that you need to spend in dice pips to buy the tile. (See board section following on how you buy tiles)

Apart from the cost, there are two things to consider when buying a tile. You need to check the roads on it. When you place a tile you need to be able to trace a road back to you palace and if you want to get the special yields on the board edges you need to connect to them by roads.

As well as the roads you also need to decide what you are going to build. Tiles have a mixture of buildings and markets on them. Buildings give you fame points when you build them but nothing else. Markets give you money when you build them and can be activated many times over during the game.

The costs of the tiles tend to reflect both the road layouts on them and the buildings or markets on them




Here is the main board and at first look it does look a bit busy and can look like the game might be quite complicated. There are actually only four different areas you can place your workers, and with three of those areas, there is only one type of action to take. So the game is surprisingly easy to understand.

A few people have commented that the game is a race game and does not really have victory points. Although I agree that it is a race, to me it has two VP tracks and the race is to progress on both of them. (Though you may decide to concentrate on one over the other).

The two tracks both start in the bottom right corner of the board. One is the fame track, this track goes clockwise around the board. The other track is the money track which goes counter clockwise around the board. The two are not equal though, one move on the fame track is equal to two moves on the money track, reflecting that it is harder to acquire fame than money.

The two tracks serve as a timer for the game. The game end is triggered when one players pieces meet or pass each other. The current round is finished (any players who have not had an action this round get one more action) and at the end of the round whoever’s pieces have gone furthest past each other is the winner. In case of a tie, it is whoever first triggered the game end conditions.

Both tracks have some special spaces on them that give you benefits when you reach them. The starting player starts on the 3 space on the money track, next player on 4 etc. All of the players have a small round token, with a 1 in a blue background on one side and two blank dice on the other side. This token starts blue side up on space 12.

When a player reaches the 12th space they take their token and advance one space on the river, (We will look at this in detail in a little while) and flip the token to the dice side and place it on space 33. If you go backwards from this space (Possible as you can spend the money) nothing happens, you do not get the bonus again or lose anything.

When you reach space 33, you again take the token flip it back to the blue side and place it on space 44, this time you get to take two dice of your choice. After you activate it on space 44 it flips one more time and moves to space 55. That is the last time you get to use it.

Players start the game with three workers and have three more available in a number of different ways. In the base game you can have a maximum of five workers. There is a worker to unlock on the money track. When you reach space 20 you get another worker to use straight away (Unless you have already unlocked the other two)

All players start on space 0 of the fame track. When you reach the 5th space you get a building upgrade. This is affects the number of fame points you get when building the type of building you choose to apply this bonus too. The 15th space on the fame track is another way you can unlock a worker.

Space 24 will move your marker up two spaces on the Karma track, we will get to that soon. The final bonus on the fame track is in space 31, this gives you one move on the river.

In the top left hand corner of the map is the Karma track. It has four rows ranging from 0 to 3. At the start of the game all of the players put a cube of their colour on the one row. At any time during your turn you can spend Karma points to flip one of your dice to its opposite side, that is a 1 can become 6, a 2 can become a 5 etc. You cannot do this action if you are on the zero row. There are a number of different ways to gain Karma.

Below that stretching to the bottom of the board and part way across to the right is the palace. Players can go here to do various actions. As well as a worker, all of the actions in the palace also require a dice.

There are six main actions in the palace and a lot of very similar actions to do with acquiring more dice. The six main actions require a dice of value 1 to 6 depending on the action. The colour of the dice does not matter.

If you use a value 1 dice, you get two things, you are start player for the next round (If no one takes this action, start player moves one player in a clockwise direction) and you get two fame points.

If you use a value 2 dice, you get to take two dice of your choice and you get a special token. These are kept face down and you draw one when you take this action. They are like the special yields on the player boards. They may give you money, fame, dice or a building upgrade. There are only eight of them, when they are all taken turn them face down, mix them up and use them again.

If you use a value 3 dice, you get a dice of your choice and move up two rows (If you are able to) on the karma track.

If you use a value 4 dice, you get a building upgrade and 3 money.

If you use a value 5 dice, you can over build a tile on your player board. This is a funny one as it very rarely gets used, but it can be a bit of a get out of jail choice if you make a mistake on your player board.

If you use a value 6 dice, you get to move 6 spaces (and only 6 spaces) on the river. Whilst to a degree it is usually good to go slowly up the river and get lots of bonuses. You may choose to use this to get an extra worker quickly and get to some potentially high scoring spaces.

Below these spaces are four spaces where you can trade one dice for two dice of a different colour. For instance you can trade a blue dice for two orange.

To the right of these spaces are four areas where you can place a worker and use no dice, to get a dice. There are two spaces for each colour. And finally on the far bottom right of the palace, is a space where you put a worker and don’t use a dice. You get 2 money and can re-roll as many of your dice as you want to.

There are three areas in the middle of the board where you can place workers. The area towards the top of the board with eight grey spaces is the quarry. Players come here to acquire tiles for their player board. The players place a worker on one of these spaces, pay the cost printed on the space (1 to 4) and can then purchase a tile, using one or more dice that equals or exceeds the value of the tile they want and which matches the colour of the dice they used.

Below the quarry are the five spaces that make up the market. There are three different goods in the game, tea, spices and silk. There are three goods specific markets and two which will accept any goods.

The first row has three markets that act identically except for the different goods that are used. To activate these markets, place a worker on the one that corresponds to the goods you wish to use. The value of the dice you use with this action (can be any colour) is up to how many markets of that type you can activate on your player board. If you have more markets than you can activate of the corresponding type you choose which ones as they do have different values. By activating a market, you gain the income from it again. You can do this multiple times during the game.

The second row contains two market spaces. To activate these you only need a worker, they do not need a dice. These spaces activate up to three different markets on you player board, again if you have more than one market of a given type, you choose which one to activate. These are pretty powerful spaces and to reflect that, the same player is not allowed to use both spaces in the same round.

Below the markets and slightly to the right are the docks. There are five space here. Like the quarry they have a cost to use, in this case from zero to two money. Winding its way through the middle of the board is a river. The players all start with their boats at the start of the river. That is the space in the bottom right corner with the boat on it. Each space on the river gives a bonus. By placing a worker in the docks a player gets to move their boat up the river and get a bonus.

You can only take the docks action though if you have a dice with a value of 3 or less. You can them move up the river up to as many spaces as the value of the dice used. While moving other players boats are skipped and not counted as spaces. The only time players can have their ships on the same space is either end of the river. If you reach the end of the river, you can no longer take the docks action.

The bonuses include money, fame, upgrades, karma, market activations, more ways of getting dice etc.

The final part of the board that is used during the game are the four buildings on the far right. They are divided into three sections. The game starts with each player having a cube in the sections marked with a 2. This represents how many points the players get when they build a tile onto their player board with this kind of building on it.

During the game there are a number of ways to get a building upgrade. When happens, the player chooses which building to upgrade and moves their cube into the next highest value space.




These are the player pieces. Each player gets their own set in their colour. In the top row of the photo you can see six workers. In the base game you will only at most get five of them, in the advanced game you get them all.

There are a number of different pieces in the second row. The first piece is the boat, not surprisingly you use this to track your progress on the river. Next is an octagonal piece (Might not be obvious, but it is), this is your marker for the money track. The slightly more than a semi-circle piece is used on the fame track. Next are five cubes. Four of these are used to track the current points value of each building and the final one is used on the karma track.

Finally on its own underneath them is the marker that is used on the money track to keep track of the bonus actions. (River move or two dice)




Here are all the dice that come with the game. They are in four colours that correspond to the building tile colours. They are regular six sided dice, but are in nice translucent colours and are very solid dice.




And here is where you store your personal supply of dice. It has room to hold ten dice. If you ever have more than that (unlikely but it could happen), you would need to discard down to ten.




Here is the first player marker. It is a man on an elephant. If someone takes the action in the palace that makes them the first player they get this, otherwise it passes clockwise around the table.




Here you see a number of tokens used in the game. The blue and grey tokens with Elephants on them are used for the three player game only. The board is actually two sided with one side for 2 player games and the other side for 3 or 4 player games. In the 3 player games, these tokens cover a space in the quarry and the harbour.

The white tokens are the bonus tokens mostly gained from the palace. There are eight different ones and if you use them all, shuffle and reuse them. The other tokens are used in the advanced version and replace the yield tokens on the player boards.

So what do I think of it?

What could be just another euro is elevated to another level by two things. The victory conditions. They are one of those things that are so simple, you wonder why nobody thought of it before. So many games these days have such complex victory conditions that often you get to the end of a game and nobody has any idea how they have done. Here, you can see at any time how you are doing. No adding up, no calculating needed at the end of the game, just look at the board and see how you did.

The other thing this game does so well is the seamless interface between the worker placement and the dice requirements. You actually notice the couple of actions that don’t need dice. I also like the way that in some cases a high value is better (The quarry) and other locations low is good (The harbour) where other locations it could be either.

There seem to be several valid paths to victory. I think using the markets to raise a lot of money is the obvious one, but remember that one fame point is equal to two money points. So a building engine is just as viable.

The river is an interesting aspect of the game as every space gives you a reward, but you can’t use a space another player is on, so timing comes into it to get the spaces you need.

In the base game you also to a degree need to prioritize how you aim to get your extra workers. Spreading yourself around between money fame and river travel will leave you chasing the game.

The components are general very good, as mentioned earlier the board initially looks a bit busy, but it works well. The two sided player boards are another good idea, allowing less experienced players to compete with experienced players. The dice as already mentioned are of a high quality.

All in all it is a very strong game that has gone over very well with everyone I have played it with. As far as I am concerned one of the stars of this years Essen crop. Highly recommended.

Título: Deutsche Rezension / Review in German
Contenido:
Das erste was einem beim Auspacken des Spiels ins Auge sticht, sind die vielen bunten würfel. Und ja, sie werden gerade im Spiel zu viert auch alle benötigt! Der erste Gedanke der uns da kam war dann auch erstmal: „Bäh, Würfel!“. Dazu muss man sagen, dass Würfelspiele bei uns nicht oberste Priorität haben, da diese doch häufig sehr glückslastig sind, was wir nicht so sehr mögen.


Aber egal, weiter auspacken und aufbauen:
Nach dem der Spielplan, die Provinzplättchen, Würfel etc. alles aufgebaut sind, kann das Spiel schon beginnen.

Ziel des Spiels ist es, so viele Punkte und Geld wie nur möglich zu erwirtschaften, um die jeweiligen Marker auf ihrer Leiste nach vorn zu bringen.
Hier haben sich die Brands mal wieder was ganz Feines ausgedacht: Das Spiel wird nicht dadurch gewonnen, dass man die meisten Punkte und das meiste Geld gesammelt hat, sondern es wird dann gewonnen, wenn die beiden Marker, die sich aufeinander zu bewegen, sich begegnen bzw. aneinander vorbeiziehen! Damit wird das Spielende ausgelöst und der Sieger steht fest.

Jeder Spieler erhält einen Würfel jeder Farbe. Damit würfelt er. Die Würfel werden dann, ohne ihren Wert zu verändern, auf die „Kali-Statue“ gelegt. Der Spieler mit dem geringsten gewürfelten Gesamtwert wird Startspieler. Er nimmt sich den Startspielerelefanten und bewegt seinen Geldmarker auf das 3. Feld der Geldleiste. Der nächste Spieler legt seinen Marker auf die 4 usw.

Um Geld oder Ruhmespunkte zu bekommen haben wir verschiedene Möglichkeiten. Aber wie bei allen guten Workerplacementspielen, haben wir mal wieder zu wenige Arbeiter.


Seine Arbeiter kann man folgendermaßen einsetzen:
Da wäre zum einen der Palast. Dieser ist in mehrere Bereich aufgegliedert. Auf der Vorterrasse kann ein Arbeiter eingesetzt werden und man erhält zwei Geld und kann beliebig viele seiner Würfel erneut würfeln.
Auf der Terrasse, die ebenfalls kostenlos ist, kann man einen weiteren Farbwürfel erhalten.

Auf den Balkonen kann einen bestimmten Farbwürfel abgeben und erhält dafür zwei Würfel einer anderen Farbe.
Und in den Gemächern muss man zu seinem Arbeiter noch einen Würfel mit einer bestimmten Augenzahl abgeben. Die Augenzahl bestimmt, welches Gemach man besuchen kann.
Wichtig ist vielleicht noch zu sagen, dass wenn man neue Würfel erhält, diese immer direkt gewürfelt werden und dann mit dieser Augenzahl auf die Kali-Statue gelegt werden.

Eine weitere Möglichkeit für Aktionen ist der Steinbruch.
Dieser ist in diesem Spiel eigentlich der wichtigste Ort, denn hier können wir Provinzplättchen kaufen um unsere Provinz auszubauen um damit sowohl unseren Ruhm als auch unseren Reichtum zu steigern.

Doch natürlich ist auch das Bauen nicht kostenlos. Schon allein das Setzen unseres Arbeiters in den Steinbruch kostet Geld. Ingesamt können bis zu 8 Arbeiter im dort platziert werden. Doch je später ein Arbeiter mit seiner Arbeit beginnt, desto mehr muss für ihn gezahlt werden.

Um nun ein Provinzplättchen zu erhalten, müssen wir Farbwürfel abgeben. Der geforderte Wert ist dabei jeweils auf den Plättchen abgegeben. Auf diesen Provinzplättchen sind Belohnungen abgebildet. Diese erhält man direkt nach dem Bau. Das können zum einen Ruhmespunkte für Gebäude oder auch Geld sein. Auf einigen dieser Plättchen sind ein oder auch mehrere Märkte abgebildet. Diese können auch im weiteren Verlauf des Spiels noch Einkommen erwirtschaften.


Durch den Ausbau unserer Provinz können wir weitere einmalige Boni, wie Ruhmespunkte, Geld oder weitere Würfel, freischalten. Diese erhält man, wenn sie über einen Weg mit dem Palast verbunden sind.

Wie gerade schon erwähnt, kann man auf dem Spielplan auch den Marktplatz besuchen um dort über die eigenen Marktplättchen Geld zu verdienen. Es gibt vier verschiedene Marktstände, jeweils einen für Seide, Tee und Gewürze sowie einen für Gemischtwaren. Setzt man nun einen Arbeiter beispielsweise auf das Feld für Tee, muss zusätzlich noch ein Farbwürfel dazugelegt werden. Die Augenanzahl bestimmt dann, wie viele eigene Märkte, die das Teesymbol besitzen, gewertet werden. Man erhält dann den aufgedruckten Geldwert jedes gewerteten Standes.

Auf dem Gemischtwarenmarkt wiederum wird jeder Rohstoff, von dem man einen Marktstand besitzt, einmal gewertet.

Als letzten Aktionspunkt gibt es noch den Hafen bzw. die damit verbundene Flussaktion. Für diese Aktion muss ebenfalls wie üblich ein Arbeiter eingesetzt werden. Zudem wird ein Würfel mit der Augenzahl 1, 2 oder 3 benötigt um das Schiff entsprechend ein bis drei Felder vorzurücken. Hat man dies getan, erhält man die dort abgebildete Belohnung. Das können beispielsweise weitere Würfel, Geld, Ruhmespunkte oder sogar ein zusätzlicher Arbeiter sein.


Überhaupt gibt es an drei Stellen die Möglichkeit weitere Arbeiter zu bekommen. Wenn man eine bestimmte Summe an Ruhmespunkten, eine bestimmte Position auf dem Fluss oder einen bestimmten Betrag auf der Geldleiste überschritten hat. Allerdings kann man nur maximal zwei Arbeiter dazubekommen. Hat man dies geschafft, wird der dritte zusätzliche Arbeiter aus dem Spiel genommen.

Pro: Wer mit der Zeit im Basisspiel sicherer geworden ist, kann auf ein weiteres Spielmodul umschwenken. Möglich ist auch, dass Neulinge die Basisvariante spielen, die Kenner die etwas anspruchsvollere Variante.

Die Spielregeln lesen sich flüssig und sind, zumindest für uns, an keiner Stelle unklar gewesen.
Man kann nicht voraussagen, wer gerade besser im Spiel steht, denn selbst wenn man auf der Leiste mit den Ruhmespunkten weit vorne steht und mit dem Geld etwas ins Hintertreffen gerät und umgekehrt, kann es plötzlich ganz schnell gehen und die Marker bewegen sich plötzlich rasend schnell aufeinander zu und ratzfatz ist das Spiel beendet.

Fazit: Die angegebene Spieldauer von 45 – 75 Minuten haben wir bislang weder im Spiel zu zweit noch zu viert geschafft. Meistens haben wir doch etwas länger gebraucht, da es einfach so viele Möglichkeiten gibt und man ständig am Abwägen war, welche Aktion man als nächstes ausführt. Vielleicht klappt es ja wenn man „Rajas of the Ganges“ häufiger gespielt hat. Bislang sind wir aber noch nicht s unter 90 Minuten geblieben.

Im Übrigen sind wir uns noch uneinig, in welche Richtung wir das Spiel einordnen würden. Ist es ein Kenner- bzw. Expertenspiel oder ist es doch auch für Familien ausgelegt? Versehen ist es mit einer Altersempfehlung ab 12 Jahren. Auch da waren wir uns nicht einig, ob es nicht sogar mit spielgeübten 10jährigen gespielt werden kann. Es gibt zwar viele Aktionsmöglichkeiten in diesem Spiel, aber diese hat man sehr schnell drauf.
Obwohl man häufig Würfel benötigt, ist „Rajas of the Ganges“ trotzdem nicht zu glückslastig, da man niedrige Zahlen genauso häufig benötigt wie hohe und wenn man doch mal eine andere Zahl benötigt, kann ein Würfel mit einem Karmapunkt verändert werden.
Alles in allem ist für uns „Rajas of the Ganges“ eine Mischung aus „Carcassonne“ und „Isle of Skye“. Zudem hat es auch ein wenig von „Auf den Spuren von Marco Polo“ und „Karuba“.
Für uns neu war hingegen die Art das Spiel zum Ende zu bringen. Das ist schon wirklich gut gelöst und unberechenbar.
Wir sind uns dennoch einig, dass „Rajas of the Ganges“ ein gelungenes und schönes Workerplacement-Spiel geworden ist, in dem es viel zu tun und besonders viel zu entdecken gibt. Egal ob zu zweit, zu dritt oder mit voller Besetzung, Spaß macht es in jedem Fall.

Título: German review / Review auf Deutsch
Contenido:
--- Thema und Spielziel ---
Im Indien der Mogulzeit wetteifern bis zu vier Rajas darum, wer seine Provinz am prachtvollsten ausbaut. Dazu werden in der eigenen Provinz Bauwerke errichtet und durch Straßen mit dem Palast verbunden. Die Mischung zwischen wirtschaftlichem Ausbau durch Märkte, auf denen Tee, Seide und Gewürze gehandelt werden, und prestigeträchtigen Gebäuden ist dabei jedem Spieler selbst überlassen. Märkte wirken sich auf das Geld des Spielers aus, Bauwerke auf seinen Ruhm. Beides wird auf je einer rund um das Spielfeld verlaufenden Leiste vermerkt, allerdings sind die Leisten in diesem Fall gegenläufig. Wer es als erster schafft, dass sich seine Markierungen für Geld und Ruhm begegnen, gewinnt das Spiel (falls nicht ein nachfolgender Spieler in der gleichen Runde seine Markierungen noch weiter aneinander vorbeibewegen kann).

--> Das Indien-Setting ist unverbraucht und der Rennspiel-Mechanismus eine willkommene Abwechslung zum vorherrschenden Die-meisten-Siegpunkte-nach-der-Endabrechnung-nach-Runde-X-Einerlei. Die beiden gegenläufigen Leisten sind eine tolle neue Idee zum Setzen des Spielziels, auch wenn sie streng genommen nichts anderes tun als eine Siegbedingung aus zwei Spielressourcen zu codieren (2xGeld + Ruhm >= 100) und geschickt zu visualisieren.


--- Mechanismen ---
Der Ausbau der eigenen Provinz erfolgt durch Landschaftsplättchen, die für Würfel einer bestimmten Farbe gekauft und dann auf dem jeweiligen Spielertableau platziert werden können. Dabei muss immer eine Wegverbindung zum eigenen Palast bestehen, damit die Märkte und Gebäude auch erreichbar sind. Am Rand des doppelseitigen Tableaus sind zudem Boni abgebildet, die bei Anschluss ans Wegenetz ausgeschüttet werden. Die eigene Provinz ist jedoch nur Nebenschauplatz, denn die zum Kauf der Plättchen nötigen Würfel, die Bauaktion an sich und diverse weitere Boni erhält man über den zentralen WorkerPlacement-Mechanismus auf dem Hauptspielbrett. Jeder beginnt dabei mit drei Arbeitern, hat anfangs also drei Aktionen pro Runde abwechselnd in Zugreihenfolge zur Verfügung. Auf beiden Siegbedingungsleisten sowie dem per Bootsfigur befahrbaren Fluß Ganges kann man weitere Arbeiter und andere Boni einsammeln, wenn man dort die dafür nötigen Felder erreicht. Erworbene Würfel werden einmalig gewürfelt, wobei alle Zahlen einen gewissen Nutzen haben und kleine Würfe nicht per se schlecht sind. Zudem gibt es die Möglichkeit, als Aktion beliebig viele seiner Würfel neu zu würfeln oder sie als Zusatzaktion auf die andere Seite zu drehen. Das dafür nötige Karma kann man aber auch an anderer Stelle oft gut gebrauchen, so dass es viele Möglichkeiten gegeneinander abzuwägen gilt und stets interessante Entscheidungen zu treffen sind. Die einzelnen Aktionen sind dabei übersichtlich und die Verschachtelungen der Effekte nicht tief, so dass immer klar ist, was wozu dient und welche Aktionen gerade möglich sind und Sinn machen.

--> Der WorkerPlacement-Mechanismus an sich ist einfach gehalten, alle Felder benötigen genau einen Arbeiter und ggf. eine kleine Geldzahlung, was besetzt ist kann erst in der nächsten Runde wieder gewählt werden. Die einzelnen Felder und Boni greifen gut ineinander, alles erscheint ausbalanciert. Man muss stets abschätzen, wie man seine Ressourcen am besten einsetzt. Die Einstiegshürde ist niedrig, alles erscheint logisch und eindeutig.


--- Komponenten ---
Die Grafik des Spiels ist sehr ansprechend und fängt das indische Flair gut ein. Die warme Farbgebung sorgt für ein einladendes Gefühl und man fühlt sich wohl auf dem Spielbrett und in seiner Provinz. Die Verarbeitung von Holzteilen, Pappplättchen, Board und Schachtel ist bestens und die Würfel sind mit ihrer farbigen Durchsichtigkeit besonders schön anzuschauen, so dass es einfach Spaß macht, mit dem Spielmaterial zu hantieren. Der Spielplan ist ebenso wie die Provinzbretter doppelseitig bedruckt, beim Plan gibt es eine Seite für 2 Spieler und eine Seite für 3 bis 4. Lediglich im Spiel zu dritt muss so mit zwei Abdeckplättchen eine kleine Anpassung vorgenommen werden. Die doppelseitigen Provinzen bieten verschiedene Boni, so dass dadurch Varianz entsteht. Das Spielmaterial ist komplett sprachneutral gehalten und die Iconographie ist klar und deutlich, so dass stets klar ist, welche Möglichkeiten man hat. Besonderes Lob verdienen das Inlay der Box, das dafür sorgt, dass alle Komponenten an ihrem Platz bleiben und die sehr schön gestalteten Rückseiten der Provinzplättchen, die zu Sortierung in zwölf Stapel dienen, so dass immer eine ausgewogene Auswahl zum Bau zur Verfügung steht.

--> Hervorragende Qualität, die dafür sorgt, dass sich das Spiel einfach gut anfühlt und ihren Teil zum Spielspaß beiträgt


--- Zugänglichkeit ---
Die viersprachige Anleitung ist sehr gut geschrieben und strukturiert, so dass keine Fragen offen bleiben. Die Regeln an sich sind nicht kompliziert und relativ schnell erklärt. Über die Verzahnung der verschiedenen Mechanismen muss man sich einen kurzen Überblick verschaffen, um sich eine grundlegende Strategie zurechtzulegen, die Verschachtelung ist aber nicht tiefgreifend, so dass dies schnell gelingt. Das Spiel ist schnell auf- und am Ende auch schnell wieder abgebaut, weil alles seinen Platz in der Box hat und es nicht wahnsinnig viel Material ist. Der Einstieg ins Spiel ist einfach, es gibt keine wichtigen Entscheidungen, die zu Beginn getroffen werden müssen, sondern man kann einfach drauf los spielen und sehen, wie sich die Dinge entwickeln.

--> Sehr gute Zugänglichkeit, die einen schnellen einfachen Einstieg ins Spiel ermöglicht


--- Glücksfaktor ---
Das Werfen der W6 unterliegt natürlich dem Zufall, je nach Spieldauer kommt jeder Spieler auf ca. 15-20 Würfe. Die höheren Ergebnisse verwendet man in der Regel zum Kaufen der Provinzplättchen, denn diese erfordern die Abgabe von Würfeln einer bestimmten Farbe in einer Mindestaugensumme (zwischen 4 und ?). Es gibt jedoch für alle Ergebnisse auch eine Verwendung bei einem Palastfeld und auch die kleinen Würfe können somit sinnvoll eingesetzt werden, wobei vor allem die Setzfelder der 2 und der 3 dabei sehr gefragt sind. Beliebig oft kann jeder Würfel für die Abgabe eines Karmapunktes auf die gegenüberliegende Seite gedreht werden und es gibt ein Setzfeld mit dem man beliebig viele seiner Würfel neu werfen kann.
Als weiterer Zufallsfaktor bleibt das Aufdecken der Provinzplättchen, für jede der vier Würfelfarben gibt es dabei aber je 17 Plättchen, die auf drei vorgefertigte Stapel verteilt sind, um eine stets ausgeglichene Mischung zu gewährleisten. Alle Farben sind dabei gleichwertig und das Stapel-System funktioniert gut, es sind immer genug Märkte und Bauwerke zur Auswahl vorhanden.

--> Der Glücksfaktor wird hier gut abgefedert, so dass er in er Regel im Laufe einer Partie nicht ins Gewicht fällt. Das Karma kann taktisch eingesetzt werden und es gehört zu den strategischen Entscheidungen, was man mit einem gewürfelten Ergebnis anfängt.


--- Varianz ---
Im einzelnen Spiel ergibt sich die Varianz vor allem durch die unterschiedlichen möglichen Strategien, was im Wesentlichen damit einhergeht, wie schnell man auf den beiden Leisten für Geld und Ruhm vorrückt und somit, welche Boni man dort durch Überschreiten gewisser Felder einsacken kann. Da durch die Siegbedingung beide Leisten wichtig sind, gibt es hier keinen Königsweg und man kann getrost verschiedene Sachen ausprobieren. Die Reise auf dem Ganges bietet eine weitere interessante Möglichkeit, gibt es doch auf jedem Feld einen Bonus, den man nur dann erhält, wenn man seine Aktion auf diesem Feld beendet. Da man mit einer Aktion 1-3 oder sogar 6 Felder auf dem Fluß vorrücken kann und gegnerische Schiffe übersprungen werden, gibt es auch hier viele Möglichkeiten abzuwägen. Bei einer schnellen Reise lässt man viele Boni zurück, die dann für dieses Spiel verloren sind, erreicht dafür aber früher den zusätzlichen Arbeiter und die später lukrativen Markt-Felder hinter der Brücke. Hier gilt es situativ zu schauen, was für die eigene Situation am besten ist.
Ein Wiederspielwert über viele Partien ergibt sich zum einen durch die Möglichkeit, die Boni der Flußfelder durch beiliegende Flußplättchen zu variieren. Zum anderen bieten auch die Provinzbretter zwei Seiten, mal mit mehr und mal mit weniger Boni am Rand, wodurch der Ausbau der eigenen Provinz wichtiger oder unwichtiger gemacht werden kann. Auch hier liegen noch Abdeckplättchen bei, mit denen man die Boni seines Provinzrandes während des Spiels anpassen kann. Zudem kann man variieren, ob die maximale Arbeiterzahl 5 oder 6 betragen soll. Durch all diese Mechanismen und die individuelle Anpassbarkeit ist dann auch das Einrichten eines Handicaps für erfahrene Spieler möglich, um Neulingen den Einstieg zu erleichtern, falls dies gewollt ist.

--> Kein Spiel läuft wie das andere, weil verfügbare Plättchen und Würfelwürfe variieren, es lassen sich verschiedene Strategien ausprobieren oder situativ auf die Ereignisse reagieren. Wiederspielwert über viele Partien ist durch den variablen Aufbau ebenfalls gegeben.


--- Interaktion ---
Wie in WorkerPlacement-Spielen üblich ergibt sich Interaktion mit den anderen Spielern allein schon durch die begrenzten Setzplätze für die Aktionen. Dass einige Aktionen da umkämpfter sind als andere, liegt in der Natur der Sache. Bei Rajas of the Ganges sind vor allem die Markt- und Palast-Aktionen umkämpft, da sie am begrenztesten zur Verfügung stehen. Hinzu kommt die Konkurrenz um attraktive Provinzplättchen, von denen immer 3 pro Würfelfarbe gleichzeitig ausliegen. Die Wertigkeit der Plättchen für die jeweiligen Spieler kann dabei unterschiedlich sein, je nachdem welche Märkte ein Spieler bereits gebaut hat, welche Wege zu Boni er aktuell benötigt und welche Gebäude er aufgewertet hat. Man muss dabei abschätzen, was die anderen Spieler wohl tun und welche Plättchen umkämpft sein könnten. Das Abschätzen ist aber durchaus möglich, weil es keine verdeckten Informationen gibt und nicht in einer Aktion Würfel und Plättchen erworben werden können. Somit ist immer klar, was sich der Gegner im nächsten Zug leisten kann und was nicht gefährdet ist. Einen direkten Angriff auf andere Spieler gibt es nicht, man kann ihnen keinerlei Ressourcen streitig machen, die sie bereits besitzen. Das führt zu einer Konkurrenz- aber keiner Konfrontationssituation.

--> Interaktion zwischen den Spielern ist vorhanden, man baut nicht einfach nur vor sich hin, sondern muss stets die Gegner im Auge behalten. Durch offene Informationen ist eine gute Planung möglich, auch wenn man nicht immer alle seine Ziele erreichen wird. Echte Konfrontation gibt es nicht, es herrscht aber steter Wettbewerb.


--- Downtime und Spiellänge ---
Wer an der Reihe ist, setzt einen seiner Arbeiter auf ein freies Aktionsfeld und führt die entsprechende Aktion aus. Das geht in der Regel ganz schnell, denn die einzelnen Aktionen sind relativ kurz gehalten. Manchmal ergeben sich kleine Verkettungen durch das Erreichen von Boni auf Leisten oder Fluß, aber auch das hält sich in Grenzen. Wer gerade nicht an der Reihe ist, kann seinen nächsten Zug in der Regel schon gut durchplanen. Weil stets sichtbar ist, was die anderen Spieler machen können, kann man sich auch schon Alternativen für den Fall überlegen, dass das gewünschte Feld dann nicht mehr frei oder das Landschaftsplättchen nicht mehr verfügbar ist. Die Downtime hält sich also in Grenzen, wenn alle konzentriert bei der Sache sind. Es gibt stets genug Optionen zum Abwägen aber eigentlich keine langen Verkettungen für endlose Grübeleien. Die Länge des Spiels an sich ist angenehm, es ist kein Mammutwerk, aber auch nicht zu kurz. Am Ende kommt man in der Regel immer schneller voran, weil durch den Ausbau von Märkten und Gebäude-Werten die Punkte einfacher aufs Konto fließen. Das Ende kann dann für manchen ganz schön überraschend kommen, wenn man denkt, man hätte noch ein, zwei Runden.

--> Angenehme Spiellänge für ein nicht abendfüllendes Spiel, Downtime hält sich in Grenzen und man kann dabei gut seine nächsten Züge planen


--- Fazit ---
Insgesamt gefällt mir Rajas of the Ganges ausgesprochen gut. Die Optimierung der eigenen Strategie ist auch für Vielspieler eine Herausforderung, der Glücksfaktor handhabbar, die Präsentation toll und Wiederspielwert, Interaktion und Downtime passen. Wer das Haar in der Suppe sucht, könnte anmerken, dass die Mechanismen etwas vom Thema abstrahiert sind und das Spiel im Prinzip auch ein anderes Szenario haben könnte, denn was die Würfel darstellen sollen und wieso ich der beste Raja bin, wenn sich meine Marker treffen, bleibt etwas abstrakt. Dieses "Problem" haben aber die meisten thematischen Spiele mit modernen Mechanismen und insgesamt ist das Thema mit Karma, Ganges und den eigenen Provinzen sehr schön umgesetzt. Die Würfelwürfe können natürlich in knappen Spielen am Ende mal entscheidend sein, aber diese gewisse Unplanbarkeit und das damit einhergehende Abwägen von Risiken bei der Entscheidungsfindung macht auch einen Teil des Spielreizes aus und der Glücksfaktor kann durch geschicktes Spielen meist ausgeglichen werden. Die gute Zugänglichkeit und der hohe Wiederspielwert machen Rajas of the Ganges zu einem Spiel, das ich der ganzen Spielerbandbreite durchweg empfehlen kann.

Título: First impressions
Contenido:
I see there are not yet any reviews on this game. So for those interested in it, I wanted to write up my first impressions after a first play. Heads-up: I'm only doing this because I enjoy it so I will be focusing on the things I like:

I have been excited about this game since I saw the video by Inka and Markus Brand. What got me curious was the idea that players score on two tracks starting at opposite ends. I think this is also what was talked about the most.

In its core, this is a worker placement game. Each player has a small number of workers they can emply to perform different tasks. Each player also has a Portuguese caravela that navigates along the river. Furthermore, each player has their own board on which they are going to place tiles in a Carcassonne-esque fashion. Finally, dice! There are a lot of dice in this game and they come in 4 different colours.

Basically what is going to happen is that players use their workers to access the market to collect money, activate the construction sight to build buildings (these are the tiles they place on their board), activate the docks to advance the ships on the river and trigger bonuses, visit characters that will grant powerful resources or actions. Here is the twist: all of these actions require you to have dice showing adequate values. For example at the docks, you can move your ships up to 3 spaces, if you have a die showing a 1, 2 or 3 (for 1, up to 2 or up to 3 spaces respecitvely).

So some of the actions (not previously mentioned) that you can do that are generic but important, consist in useing your workers to obtain new dice and many of the bonuses too give you new dice.

Building tiles also requires specific dice values (or a combined value among 2 or more dice) and on top of that require the dice to be of a specific colour depending on the type of tile you want to build.

I mentionned the two tracks. One is for prestige points and the other for money. So the money track is really only to keep track of your monetary wealth and everytime you need to pay for an action, you simply go back that many spaces on the money track. The game ends in the round that one player has their prestige point marker meet the money marker. So a player could be investing a lot more in money than in prestige, as long as their markers meet sooner than any other players' they could actually win with fewer prestige points than anybody else. Whilst most people find this orignal I actually don't see it the same way: many games convert money into points at the end which can often result in unexpected victories.

Neverhteless, the money track can still be used to activate certain bonuses as the marker progresses, and this is something that isn't easily accomplished by collecting coin tokens instead of keeping track of money on a... track...

Anyways, there is a lot going on in this game and there are many options for players to decide on each turn. This feels like a really solid euro game that takes ideas from different games and blends them together really well. The Carcassonne style elemt is a really good example: players try to connect their palaces to the outscurts to activate bonuses (seen in Android) whilst simultanesouly trying to score prestige points with buildings they are strong on or adding markets to generate money.

I really enjoyed the game and can't wait to play again soon.

Whilst most people will mention the two tracks as the one thing that sets this game apart, to me actually the one element I like best is the dice. I have seen games that use dice as the workers (The Voyages of Marco Polo or Quantum Cubes) and of course there are plenty of worker placement games that use meeples to activate the spaces. However, Rajas of the Ganges uses a unique mechanic that mixes the workers with dice where the dice create opportunities or challenges in how the workers can be used. To add to that tension, unlike the workers, which return to you at the end of a round, the players' dice pool does not refresh and players have to simultaneously manage the dice quantity that is available to them as well as the dice "quality" that allows them to perform the actions they need each turn. This is definitely my favourite part about this game.

I hope you find this short review helpful.
